  \section*{Introduction}
  The program \ty{fintac} takes as input a phylogenetic tree and a
  target taxon. It then finds the target clade in the tree. The usecase
  we have in mind is centered on finding strains in bacterial species
  trees. Consider as example target the \emph{Escherichia coli} strain
  O157:H7 with taxon-ID 83334. The task is to find its clade in a
  phylogeny of all \emph{E. coli} genomes. Apart from O157:H7, this
  phylogeny contains taxa classified as other strains, we call them
  \emph{neigobors}. In addition, there are taxa classified as
  \emph{E. coli}, taxon-ID 562. These may be targets \emph{or}
  neighbors, we call them \emph{unknowns}. The analysis relies on these
  three types of leaves being distinguishable, taxonomic targets,
  neighbors, and unknowns. In addition, the internal nodes of the
  phylogeny have unique labels. The program \ty{fintac} then looks for
  the node that maximizes the split between targets on the one hand, and
  neighbors and unknowns on the other.

  To find this node, let $n_{\rm t}$, $n_{\rm n}$, and $n_{\rm u}$ be
  the number of taxonomic targets, neighbors, and unknowns in a
  phylogeny with $n=n_{\rm t}+n_{\rm n}+n_{\rm u}$ leaves. We further
  call $v_{\rm t}$, $v_{\rm n}$, and $v_{\rm u}$ the number of targets,
  neighbors, and unknowns in the clade rooted on some node $v$. If the
  taxonomy always agreed with the phylogeny, we could just look for the
  node that contains all targets and no neighbors or unknowns, $v_{\rm
    t}=n_{\rm t}$ and $v_{\rm n}=v_{\rm u}=0$. However, as is
  well-known, there is often no phylogenetic node that splits the sample
  into pure taxonomic targets and neighbors. So we define a split score
  per node, $s(v)$, and pick the node with the largets split score.

  The split score should increase with the number of targets inside $v$,
  and decrease with the number of neighbors and unknowns. It should also
  be bounded by 0 and some maximum, we pick 100. To ensure the lower
  bound, we further define $v^{\ast}_{\rm n}$ as the number of neighbors
  \emph{outside} of $v$. Similarly, $v^{\ast}_{\rm u}$ is the number of
  unknowns outside of $v$. Our idea is to maximize the sum of targets
  inside and non-targets outside, yielding our first draft split score,
  \[
  s_{\rm d}(v)=v_{\rm t} + v^{\ast}_{\rm n} + v^{\ast}_{\rm u}.
  \]

  In the above draft we treat unknowns like targets and neighbors by
  entering them linearly. However, positive identification as target or
  neighbor is much stronger information than ``unknown''. So we enter
  the unknowns outside logarithmically to get a revised draft,
  \[
  s_{\rm d}(v)=v_{\rm t} + v^{\ast}_{\rm n} + \log(v^{\ast}_{\rm u} + 1).
  \]

  To ensure the upper bound, we divide our draft split score by its
  maximum and multiply it by 100 to get the final version,
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:spl}
    s(v)=\frac{v_{\rm t} + v^{\ast}_{\rm n} +\log(v^{\ast}_{\rm
        u}+1)}{n_{\rm t} + n_{\rm n} + \log(n_{\rm u}+1)}\times 100.
  \end{equation}

  In the worst case, a focal node contains no targets and all neighbors
  and unknowns. In that case the three terms in the numerator are zero,
  so the split score is also zero. In the best case, the focal node
  contains all targets in a vanishingly short tree and no neighbors or
  unknowns, so the split score is 100. In other words, the split score
  ranges from 0 to 100, $0\le s(v)\le 100$, as intended.

  The program \ty{fintac} allows targets, neighbors, and unknowns to be
  specified as regular expressions. As usual, such regular expressions
  may refer to any part of a leaf label, and by default we make no
  assumptions about the format of the leaf labels. However, we often
  follow the convention that a leaf consists of a prefix taxon-ID that
  is separated from the rest of the label by an underscore. In that case
  a taxon might be in the taxonomic subtree rooted on a target taxon,
  which should count as a match.

  Consider as example target \emph{Escherichia coli} O157:H7, which has
  taxon-ID 83334. Any leaf with prefix ``83334\_'' is now a match. In
  addition, leaves with prefix ``155864\_'' should also match, as this
  is the taxon-ID of the O157:H7 strain EDL933. There are over 130
  O157:H7 substrains with their own taxon-IDs. To ensure such substrains
  are classified correctly, we implement ``hierarchical matching''. The
  user can activate hierarchical matching for targets and neighbors by
  running \ty{fintac} with the Neighbors database in addition to the
  phylogeny. For unknowns herarchical matching is not appropriate, as
  they are typically above the targets in the taxonomic tree, e. g. at
  species rank, which would make every taxon below that rank an unknown.
